\title{SF-Detector: An Efficient and Accurate Method for Detecting Steady Flows in Network Traffic}

\author[csust]{Zhenxing Chen}
\ead{1006503012@qq.com}

\affiliation[csust]{organization={School of Computer and Communication Engineering, Changsha University of Science and Technology},
            city={Changsha},
            country={China}}

\begin{abstract}
Steady flows refer to packet flows whose packet arrival rates remain relatively steady across multiple consecutive time windows. Such flows represent sustained data transmission behaviors of network applications, and their detection is valuable for bandwidth management, resource planning, and cache optimization. Existing methods require storing per-flow information across multiple time windows to track flow stability, resulting in high memory overhead and low throughput. To address these issues, we propose an efficient and accurate method for detecting steady flows in network traffic called SF-Detector, which separates the filtering and recording functions of packet flows, enabling efficient detection and reporting of steady flows. In particular, SF-Detector consists of a filtering part that employs three rotating counting bloom filters to screen out potentially steady flows, and a recording part that adopts a hash table to track and verify potentially steady flows. As for the recording part, we define a calculation-friendly flow stability metric as the ratio of the maximum of packet arrival frequencies across consecutive time windows to their minimum, to efficiently determine steady flows. Additionally, we also design a flow replacement strategy to accommodate each new potentially-steady flow, by evicting the flow with the minimum stability counter in its mapped bucket if the bucket is already full. Furthermore, we provide a theoretical error bound for frequency estimation in the filtering part and false negatives of steady flow detection in the recording part. Finally, we validate the performance of our proposed SF-Detector through extensive experiments on three real-world datasets. Experimental results indicate that SF-Detector achieves a precision of 97\%, a recall rate of 99\%, and a throughput of 40 MIPS, outperforming state-of-the-art methods.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Network traffic measurement \cite{b1,b2,b3} plays a vital role in the traffic engineering and performance management of large-scale dynamic network traffic \cite{b4,b5,b6,b7}. It provides critical information such as traffic volume statistics, flow distributions, and traffic patterns for applications including bandwidth management \cite{b8}, traffic engineering, and resource allocation \cite{b9}. These applications demand high measurement accuracy to ensure reliable decision-making. However, it is impractical to store all data due to the high speed and massive scale of modern network traffic, forcing measurement to rely on sampling or approximation techniques rather than exact recording. This makes achieving both high-precision and low-overhead network measurement particularly challenging. To address this challenge, Sketch-based techniques achieve accurate measurement with low memory consumption through probabilistic algorithms \cite{b10,b11}. Therefore, they are widely applied in network measurement. These techniques have demonstrated excellent performance in tasks such as flow cardinality estimation \cite{b12}, flow frequency estimation \cite{b13}, heavy hitter identification \cite{b14}, quantile estimation \cite{b15}, and entropy estimation \cite{b16}. Besides these traditional measurement tasks, researchers have recently extended Sketch-based techniques to detecting novel flow patterns, including burst flows \cite{b17}, periodic flows \cite{b18}, persistent flows \cite{b19}, and steady flows.


In this paper, steady flows are defined as packet flows whose arrival frequencies remain around a fixed value across multiple consecutive time windows, while tolerating minor fluctuations within a controllable range. This definition focuses on the overall stability of packet flow frequency rather than instantaneous values, achieving both practical applicability and detection robustness. The detection of steady flows provides fundamental support for multiple domains: (a) bandwidth management: it enables accurate traffic prediction and facilitates proactive bandwidth allocation \cite{b20}; (b) cache optimization: it helps detect content with sustained access demand and improves cache hit rates \cite{b21}; (c) quality of service: it supports priority scheduling for latency-sensitive applications such as video conferencing and online gaming.

So far, there has been little research on steady flow detection in network traffic measurement. Although On-Off Sketch \cite{b22} employs an On-Off mechanism that increments counters at most once per time window to track persistent flows appearing across multiple windows, it only records flow presence rather than packet arrival frequencies, making it unable to assess arrival rate stability. While PISketch \cite{b19} utilizes a reward-penalty system combined with a Weight Sketch structure to identify persistent and infrequent flows by dynamically managing flow weights across time windows, it focuses on flow occurrence patterns rather than frequency stability. These methods are designed for persistent flow detection rather than steady flow detection, as they cannot evaluate whether a flow's arrival rate remains around a fixed value. SteadySketch \cite{b23} directly addresses steady flow detection by storing per-flow frequency information across multiple time windows and performing variance calculations to assess stability. However, this approach leads to high memory overhead and low processing throughput, making it impractical for high-speed network environments. Consequently, it is imperative to develop an efficient and accurate method that can detect steady flows with low memory overhead and high throughput.

To address the aforementioned problems, we propose SF-Detector, an efficient and accurate method for detecting steady flows in network traffic, which separates the filtering and recording functions of packet flows. Specifically, the filtering part employs three rotating counting bloom filters to track packet arrival frequencies across three consecutive time windows in a circular manner, effectively screening out potentially steady flows before they enter the recording part. This results in only a small number of potentially steady flows entering the recording part, significantly reducing memory overhead. The recording part is implemented as a hash table, where each bucket contains multiple cells to store essential information of potentially steady flows, including the flow identifier, the current-window frequency, the maximum and minimum frequencies across all recorded windows, and a consecutive-stability counter. With this information, SF-Detector reports steady flows at the end of each time window based on a stability metric which is defined as the ratio of the maximum of packet arrival frequencies across consecutive time windows to their minimum, to efficiently determine steady flows, avoiding complex variance calculations, enhancing the detection efficiency of steady flows. Additionally, we design a flow replacement strategy, which accommodates each new potentially-steady flow, by evicting the flow with the minimum consecutive-stability counter in its mapped bucket if the bucket is already full, enhancing the detection accuracy of steady flows. The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Proposing an efficient and accurate method for detecting steady flows in network traffic called SF-Detector, which adopts a filtering part to screen out potentially steady flows, and a recording part to track and verify potentially steady flows, achieving low overhead and high accuracy.
    \item Defining a calculation-friendly flow stability metric as the ratio of the maximum of packet arrival frequencies across consecutive time windows to their minimum, for efficient determination of steady flows.
    \item Designing an effective flow replacement strategy that evicts the flow with the minimum stability counter in a full bucket to accommodate newly-arrived potentially-steady flow, for accurate detection of steady flows.
    \item Deriving theoretical error bounds for the flow frequency estimation in filtering part and the false negative rate of steady-flow detection in the recording part to verify the Accuracy of SF-Detector.
\end{itemize}

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 defines steady flows and discusses related work in network measurement. Section 3 provides a detailed description of the SF-Detector and derives a theoretical analysis of the error bounds. Section 4 describes the experimental setup and results, including comparisons with existing solutions. Finally, Section 5 concludes the paper.

\section{Definition of Steady Flow and Related Work}

\subsection{Definition of Steady Flow}
Suppose there exists a sequential packet flow sequence $S=\{e_{1},e_{2},e_{3},...\}$, which is divided into multiple fixed-size time windows $w_1, w_2, w_3, ...$. For the packet flow $e$, let its arrival frequencies in each time window be $f_{1},f_{2},f_{3},...$. In SF-Detector, a flow is first pre-verified in the filtering part over a fixed number of consecutive time windows denoted by $L_f$. After that, it enters the recording part and is further confirmed there. If the flow $e$ satisfies the following condition in the recording part:

\begin{equation}
    \begin{cases}
    C_{st} \ge \Gamma \\[6pt]
    F_{\max} \le (1+\beta) \cdot \dfrac{F_{\max} + F_{\min}}{2} \\[8pt]
    F_{\min} \ge (1-\beta) \cdot \dfrac{F_{\max} + F_{\min}}{2}
    \end{cases}
    \label{eq:stability}
\end{equation}

where $\beta$ indicates the fluctuation tolerance threshold for steady flow detection, and $\Gamma$ denotes the threshold of additional consecutively stable time windows required in the recording part. In our design, the filtering part uses a fixed pre-verification stage spanning three consecutive time windows, i.e., $L_f = 3$. Therefore, a reported steady flow has remained stable for at least $L_f + \Gamma$ consecutive time windows in total. \subsection{Related Work}
Frequency estimation is a fundamental function of steady flow detection. Previous frequency estimation methods are mostly designed based on probabilistic data structures, such as Count-Min Sketch \cite{b24} and Count Sketch \cite{b25}. Count-Min Sketch is composed of a $d \times w$ two-dimensional array and $d$ mutually independent hash functions. When a packet arrives, it maps the packet into $d$ counters by hashing and increments them by 1. As for the query of a flow, the minimum of its mapped counters is reported as its estimated frequency. Count Sketch is similar to Count-Min Sketch, but it incorporates an additional bool function to increment or decrement the mapped counters of a packet. As for the query of a flow, the median of its mapped counters is taken as its estimated frequency. Bloom filter \cite{b26} is a classical data structure designed to efficiently handle set membership queries. Some researchers \cite{b27} curtailed false positives and accelerated queries by adapting the number of hash functions on-line, attaining superior scalability in massive-data environments. An improved counting bloom filter \cite{b28} was proposed for counting data streams. However, these methods can only estimate the frequency of a flow in entire data stream, but cannot track the stability of flow arrival rates across consecutive time windows. Consequently, traditional frequency estimation methods cannot be directly applied to detect steady flows.

In recent years, some researchers have proposed new methods to detect flows with temporal characteristics. Sliding Sketch \cite{b29} proposed a time-zone-based method that dynamically processes data streams in sliding windows. However, Sliding Sketch focuses on sliding window queries rather than flow stability detection. Persistent flow detection shares conceptual ground with steady flow detection in their demand for continuity. On-Off Sketch \cite{b22} was proposed to detect persistent flows, tailoring it for fast and accurate persistence detection. However, On-Off Sketch only tracks whether a flow exists in each window, not its frequency stability. PISketch \cite{b19} utilizes a reward-penalty system to detect persistent and infrequent flows by dynamically managing flow weights across time windows. However, it focuses on flow occurrence patterns rather than frequency stability. In summary, existing temporal flow detection methods focus on different characteristics (sliding windows, bursts, periodicity, or persistence) rather than the stability of arrival rates.

Research on steady-flow detection remained sparse. SteadySketch \cite{b23} is a sketch-based structure that coupled Bloom filtering with counter-rebirth techniques to yield a memory-efficient detection mechanism. However, the scheme's computational complexity climbed sharply under high-speed traffic: every variance calculation and stability decision imposed a heavy arithmetic load. Moreover, the absence of an efficient pre-filtering step let large volumes of unsteady flows advance to later stages, needlessly swelling the system's computational and storage burden. In summary, existing detection methods cannot detect steady flows at high accuracy with low memory and computational overheads.

\section{The Design of SF-Detector}

\subsection{The Framework of SF-Detector}
Considering that non-continuous flows account for the majority of packet flows in network traffic, we construct the framework of SF-Detector in Figure \ref{fig:framework}, which separates the processing of packet flows into a filtering part and a recording part. The filtering part employs three rotating counting bloom filters to track flow frequencies across three consecutive time windows and screen out potentially steady flows. We propose a rolling update mechanism for the filtering part to efficiently maintain flow frequency information with minimal memory overhead. The recording part adopts a hash table to precisely track and verify potentially steady flows. We design a flow replacement strategy for the recording part to accommodate each new potentially-steady flow, by evicting the flow with the minimum consecutive-stability counter in its mapped bucket if the bucket is already full, enhancing the detection accuracy of steady flows. At the end of each time window, we report steady flows and clear out outdated information and flows that are no longer steady.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centerline{\includegraphics[width=2.5in, trim=10 10 10 10, clip]{The framework of SF-Detector.pdf}}
\caption{The framework of the SF-Detector.}
\label{fig:framework}
\end{figure}

For each arrived packet in the flow $f$, we first look up the recording part. If $f$ has already been recorded, we only update its current-window frequency $F_{cur}$. Otherwise, we insert the packet into the current counting bloom filter $CBF_1$ and immediately estimate the frequencies of $f$ in recent three time windows by its mapped counters in $CBF_1$, $CBF_2$, and $CBF_3$. If the estimated frequencies are all non-zero, the flow $f$ is considered to have appeared in the three time windows. If they also satisfy the stability condition over these three time windows, we regard $f$ as a potentially steady flow and insert it into the recording part. For each newly inserted flow $f$, we set $F_{cur}$ to the estimated frequency of $f$ by $CBF_1$ for the current time window, set $F_{\max}$ and $F_{\min}$ to the maximum and minimum estimated frequencies by $CBF_1$, $CBF_2$, and $CBF_3$, respectively, and set $C_{st}$ to 0. At the end of each time window, we traverse the recording part to update $F_{\max}$ and $F_{\min}$ in each recorded flow with its frequency in the time window, and check whether the flow still satisfy the stability condition. If not, we remove the flow. Otherwise, we increment its $C_{st}$ by 1. If $C_{st}$ reaches the threshold $\Gamma$, the flow is reported as a steady flow. For each retained flow, we reset its $F_{cur}$ to 0. Finally, we apply logical rolling operations to the three counting bloom filters for next time window.

\subsection{Filtering Part}

\textbf{Data structure:} The filtering part is comprised of three counting bloom filters, $CBF_1$, $CBF_2$, and $CBF_3$, each containing $w$ counters in Figure \ref{fig:filter}. The three filters track packet arrivals across consecutive time windows. For this purpose, the three filters share a set of $k$ mutually independent hash functions, $H_1(\cdot), H_2(\cdot), \ldots, H_k(\cdot)$. These functions map each flow to $k$ counter positions in the current-window $CBF$, and the other two $CBF$s use the same positions for the same flow. Each counter records the cumulative packet arrival count of all flows hashed to that counter position. Specifically, $CBF_1$, $CBF_2$, and $CBF_3$ are used to estimate the frequency of a flow in the current, previous, and oldest of the three consecutive time windows, respectively.
 

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centerline{\includegraphics[width=1\textwidth, trim=10 10 10 10, clip]{Non-continuous flow filtering structure.pdf}}
\caption{The filtering part of non-continuous flows.}
\label{fig:filter}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Initialization:} Initially, we set all counters in $CBF_1$, $CBF_2$, and $CBF_3$ to zero.

\textbf{Packet processing:} For an arrived packet in the flow $f$, we first look up the recording part. If the lookup fails, we map the packet to $k$ counters in the $CBF_1$ by $k$ hash functions $H_1(\cdot), \ldots, H_k(\cdot)$ and increment the minimum mapped counter by 1. Then we query the counters at the k positions mapped from the flow $f$ in each of the three counting bloom filters. Then we take the minimum mapped counter in each filter as the estimated frequency of the flow $f$ in the time window to determine whether the flow $f$ is a potentially steady flow. If the estimated frequencies of the flow $f$ are all non-zero and satisfy the stability condition defined in Eq.~(\ref{eq:stability}), we insert flow $f$ into the recording part.

\textbf{Rolling updates:} At the end of each time window, we clear out counters of the oldest-window filter to record packet arrivals in new window. 

\textbf{A running example:} Figure \ref{fig:filter} illustrates a running example of the filtering part. Assume that the number of hash functions $k = 3$, the fluctuation tolerance $\beta = 0.2$. The detection of $f_1$, $f_2$, and $f_3$ at the end of the time window can be described as follows.

1) For $f_1$, we obtain its mapped counters in the three counting bloom filters by hashing and use the minimum mapped counter in each filter as the window frequency estimate. The estimated frequencies across three windows are 5, 4, and 5 respectively. All estimated frequencies are non-zero and satisfy the stability condition. Thus, $f_1$ is detected as a potentially steady flow and transferred to the recording part.

2) For $f_2$, we find that $CBF_3[H_1(f_2)] = 0$. Therefore, the minimum estimated frequency of $f_2$ in the oldest window is 0, indicating that $f_2$ did not appear continuously across three windows. Thus, $f_2$ is filtered out as a non-continuous flow.

3) For $f_3$, the estimated frequencies across three windows are 3, 4, and 8 respectively. Although all estimated frequencies are non-zero, the frequency variation exceeds the tolerance threshold $\beta$. Thus, $f_3$ is filtered out as an unstable flow.

\subsection{Recording Part}

\textbf{The data structure of the recording part:} As shown in Figure \ref{fig:record_table}, the recording part is implemented as a hash table indexed by a hash function $h(\cdot)$. It consists of $M$ buckets $A[1], A[2], ..., A[M]$, each containing $n$ cells. Each cell stores the information of one flow, including Flow ID, $F_{cur}$, $F_{\max}$, $F_{\min}$, and $C_{st}$. To save memory, Flow ID is represented only by the fingerprint of a flow identified by the standard 5-tuple: source IP address, destination IP address, source port, destination port, and protocol type.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centerline{\includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth, trim=15 15 15 15, clip]{Data Structure of the Steady flow Recording Table.pdf}}
\caption{Data Structure of the Steady Flow Recording Table.}
\label{fig:record_table}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Initialization:} Initially, we set all fields to 0 or NULL.

\textbf{Packet processing:} For an arriving packet from flow $f$, we first look up $f$ in the recording part. If $f$ has already been recorded there, we increment its current-window frequency $F_{cur}$ by 1. Otherwise, we insert the packet into the filtering part.

\textbf{The insertion of a potentially steady flow:} After the end-of-window processing of the recorded flows, if the flow $f$ is detected as a potentially steady flow by the filtering part, we look up the recording part for an empty cell in bucket $A[h(f)]$ and place the flow $f$ into it. The three verified windows in the filtering part serve only as pre-verification, while $C_{st}$ counts additional stable windows accumulated after the flow enters the recording part. There are two cases:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Case 1:} There is an empty cell in the bucket $A[h(f)]$. In this case, we insert the flow $f$ into the empty cell. Specifically, we set Flow ID as $f$, $F_{cur}$ as the current estimated frequency of $f$ in $CBF_1$, $F_{\max}$ as the maximum of the estimated frequencies in $CBF_1$, $CBF_2$, and $CBF_3$, $F_{\min}$ as the minimum of these frequencies, and $C_{st}$ as 0.
    \item \textbf{Case 2:} If there is no empty cell in the bucket $A[h(f)]$, we find the flow with the smallest $C_{st}$ and replace it with the flow $f$. The new inserted flow is initialized in the same way. This replacement strategy ensures that flows with longer stability history are preserved.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{The report of steady flows:} At the end of each time window, before inserting newly verified flows from the filtering part, we traverse each existing cell in the recording part to report current steady flows. For each non-empty cell, we first use the completed current-window frequency $F_{cur}$ to update $F_{\max}$ and $F_{\min}$, and then check whether the stability condition is still satisfied. The end-of-window processing proceeds as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Case 1:} If the updated frequency information does not satisfy the stability condition, the flow in this cell is no longer steady, and we reset all information in this cell.
    \item \textbf{Case 2:} If the stability condition is satisfied, we increment $C_{st}$ by 1. If the updated $C_{st}$ reaches the threshold $\Gamma$, the flow is reported as a steady flow. In either case, $F_{cur}$ is reset to 0 for the next time window.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{A running example:} Figure \ref{fig:record_table} illustrates a running example of the recording part. Assume that the fluctuation tolerance is set to $\beta = 0.2$, the threshold of additional consecutive stable windows in the recording part is $\Gamma = 3$, and the current time window is $W_{cur} = 5$. The insertion of $f_1$, $f_2$, and $f_3$, together with the end-of-window processing, is described as follows.

1) To insert a packet in $f_1$, it has been recorded in its directly mapped bucket. Therefore, we simply increment the corresponding $F_{cur}$ by 1. The update of $F_{\max}$ and $F_{\min}$ is deferred until the end of the current time window.

2) To insert $f_2$ detected as a potentially steady flow by the filtering part, we find an empty cell in the bucket $A[h(f_2)]$. We insert $(f_2, 15, 15, 15, 0)$ into the empty cell, where 15 is the estimated frequency from the filtering part.

3) To insert $f_3$ detected as a potentially steady flow, there is no empty cell in the bucket $A[h(f_3)]$. We select the flow with the smallest $C_{st}$ in the bucket and replace it with $(f_3, 12, 12, 12, 0)$.

4) At the end of the time window, we traverse each cell to check stability. For $f_4$ with updated $F_{\max} = 20$ and $F_{\min} = 8$, the frequency variation exceeds the tolerance threshold $\beta$. Thus, we reset all fields in this cell.

5) For $f_5$ with updated $F_{\max} = 18$, $F_{\min} = 15$, and $C_{st} = 2$, the stability condition is satisfied. Thus, we increment $C_{st}$ to 3, report $f_5$ as a steady flow, and reset $F_{cur}$ to 0. Together with the three pre-verified windows in the filtering part, $f_5$ has remained stable for at least $L_f + \Gamma = 6$ consecutive windows in total.

6) For $f_6$ with updated $F_{\max} = 25$, $F_{\min} = 22$, and $C_{st} = 4$, the stability condition is still satisfied. Thus, we increment $C_{st}$ by 1, continue to report $f_6$ as a steady flow, and reset $F_{cur}$ to 0.

\subsection{Theoretical Analysis}
In this section, we perform the theoretical analysis of SF-Detector. We first derive the error bound of flow frequency estimation in the filtering part in Section 3.4.1. Then, we provide an upper-bound analysis of replacement-induced false negatives in the recording part in Section 3.4.2. Finally, we provide the space and time complexities of SF-Detector in Section 3.4.3.

\subsubsection{The error bounds of flow frequency estimation in the filtering part}

\begin{theorem}
Suppose the filtering part consists of $w$ counters and $k$ independent hash functions. Let $S$ be a sequence of network traffic with $W$ packets. For a flow $g$ in the filtering part, let $\hat{f}$ be its estimated frequency, and let $f$ be its true frequency. Given a small variable $\delta$ ($\delta > 0$), we can infer the error bound of flow frequency estimation in the filtering part as
\begin{equation}
    P(\hat{f} - f > \delta \cdot W) \le \left(\frac{1}{\delta \cdot w}\right)^k.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\textit{Proof.} As for any flow $g$, let $X_j$ ($1 \le j \le k$) be its mapped counter in the filtering part by the $j$-th hash function. We can formulate $X_j$ as
\begin{equation}
    X_j = f + \sum_{g_i \ne g} \mathbb{I}(h_j(g_i) = h_j(g)) \cdot f_{g_i},
\end{equation}
where $g_i$ refers to a flow that is different from the flow $g$ in the filtering part with the frequency $f_{g_i}$; $h_j(\cdot)$ represents the $j$-th hash function; $\mathbb{I}(\cdot)$ is a boolean function.

Since there are a total of $W$ packets, other flows contain $W - f$ packets. Suppose that all flows are randomly mapped into $w$ counters, we can obtain the expectation of the frequency estimation error of the flow $g$ caused by other flows as
\begin{equation}
    E\left[\sum_{g_i \ne g} \mathbb{I}(h_j(g_i) = h_j(g)) \cdot f_{g_i}\right] = \frac{W - f}{w}.
\end{equation}

Since all hash functions are independent of each other, we can apply the Markov inequality to derive the error bound of flow frequency estimation at the $j$-th mapped counter of flow $g$ as
\begin{equation}
    P\left(\sum_{g_i \ne g} \mathbb{I}(h_j(g_i) = h_j(g)) \cdot f_{g_i} > \delta \cdot W\right) \le \frac{W - f}{\delta \cdot W \cdot w} < \frac{1}{\delta \cdot w}.
\end{equation}

Since the filtering part takes the minimal mapped counter of a flow as its estimated frequency, we can express the estimated frequency of the flow $g$ as
\begin{equation}
    \hat{f} = \min_{1 \le j \le k} X_j.
\end{equation}

Because the $k$ hash functions are independent, the probability that all mapped counters exceed the error threshold equals the product of the individual probabilities. Therefore, we can derive the error bound of flow frequency estimation in the filtering part as
\begin{equation}
    P(\hat{f} - f > \delta \cdot W) \le \left(\frac{1}{\delta \cdot w}\right)^k.
\end{equation}
\hfill $\square$

\subsubsection{The error bounds of false negatives of steady flow detection in the recording part}

In the recording part, a truly steady flow may fail to be reported if it is evicted before its $C_{st}$ reaches the reporting threshold $\Gamma$. We next bound the per-window replacement probability and the resulting false negative probability before reporting.

\begin{theorem}
Suppose the recording part consists of $M$ buckets, each of which contains $n$ cells. The recording part records $F$ flows, among which there are $S$ flows whose $C_{st}$ values are lower than the threshold $\Gamma$. Then the probability that a recorded flow $g$ with $C_{st} < \Gamma$ is replaced in a time window is upper bounded by
\begin{equation}
    P_{\mathrm{rep}} = \frac{1}{M} \cdot \left(\frac{e^{-\lambda} \lambda^n}{n!}\right)^3 \cdot \frac{S}{F \cdot n},
\end{equation}
where $\lambda = \frac{F}{M}$. Since a steady flow is reported only after accumulating at least $\Gamma$ additional stable time windows in the recording part, $P_{\mathrm{rep}}$ serves as an upper bound on the replacement-induced false negative probability per window. Moreover, the probability that such a flow is replaced at least once before reaching the reporting threshold is upper bounded by
\begin{equation}
    P_{\mathrm{FN}} \le 1 - (1 - P_{\mathrm{rep}})^{\Gamma} \le \Gamma \cdot P_{\mathrm{rep}}.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\textit{Proof.} We first analyze the insertion and update process in the recording part:

\textbf{I:} As for any flow $g$, if it has been recorded in the recording part, we directly increase its counter value by 1.

\textbf{II:} If the flow $g$ is not recorded in the recording part and there is an empty cell in its directly mapped bucket, the flow $g$ is inserted into this empty cell.

\textbf{III:} If the flow $g$ is replaced by a new potentially-steady flow $g'$, the information of $g$ is removed from the recording part. If $g$ is truly steady but has not yet reached the reporting threshold $\Gamma$, this replacement leads to a false negative.

In summary, the replacement-induced false negative in the recording part occurs only when the following events happen simultaneously: 1) Event $A$: The flow $g'$ is mapped to the same bucket as the flow $g$. 2) Event $B$: There are no empty cells in the bucket. 3) Event $C$: The $C_{st}$ of the flow $g$ is the smallest in the bucket. 4) Event $D$: The flow $g$ is successfully replaced by the flow $g'$.

Since the above events are independent of each other, the probability of the flow $g$ being replaced is
\begin{equation}
    P = P(A \cap B \cap C \cap D) = P(A) \cdot P(B) \cdot P(C) \cdot P(D).
\end{equation}

Owing to that each flow is randomly mapped into the recording part by hashing, we can consider the insertion of each flow as an independent random event. Consequently, we can infer that occurrence probability of the event $A$ as
\begin{equation}
    P(A) = \frac{1}{M}.
\end{equation}

Suppose that each flow will be randomly inserted into any bucket, the number of flows recorded in each bucket approximately follows a Poisson distribution with parameter $\lambda = \frac{F}{M}$. Hence, the occurrence probability of the event $B$ is approximated by
\begin{equation}
    P(B) = \left(\frac{e^{-\lambda} \lambda^n}{n!}\right)^3.
\end{equation}

Because there are $S$ flows with $C_{st}$ values lower than the threshold $\Gamma$, the probability that the flow $g$ has the smallest $C_{st}$ in the bucket is
\begin{equation}
    P(C) = \frac{S}{F \cdot n}.
\end{equation}

The occurrence probability of the event $D$ is
\begin{equation}
    P(D) = 1.
\end{equation}

Based on the above discussion, we can derive the probability that flow $g$ is replaced by a potentially steady flow $g'$ in the current time window as
\begin{equation}
    P = P(A \cap B \cap C \cap D) = \frac{1}{M} \cdot \left(\frac{e^{-\lambda} \lambda^n}{n!}\right)^3 \cdot \frac{S}{F \cdot n}.
\end{equation}

The flow $g$ is replaced because its $C_{st}$ is the smallest. Consequently, its per-window replacement probability satisfies $P_{\mathrm{rep}} = P$, and the false negative probability before reaching the reporting threshold is upper bounded by $P_{\mathrm{FN}} \le 1 - (1 - P_{\mathrm{rep}})^{\Gamma} \le \Gamma \cdot P_{\mathrm{rep}}$.
\hfill $\square$

\subsubsection{Space and time complexities}

\textbf{Space complexity:} SF-Detector consists of a filtering part and a recording part. The filtering part contains $3 \times w$ counters, where $w$ is the number of counters in each counting bloom filter. Meanwhile, the recording part contains $M \cdot n$ cells, where $M$ is the number of buckets and $n$ is the number of cells in each bucket. Let the size of each counter in the filtering part and each cell in the recording part be $S_1$ and $S_2$, respectively. Then the total memory size of SF-Detector is $3 \cdot w \cdot S_1 + M \cdot n \cdot S_2$. In SF-Detector, each counter in the filtering part is set to 8 bits. Each cell in the recording part consists of five fields: Flow ID, $F_{cur}$, $F_{\max}$, $F_{\min}$, and $C_{st}$. Flow ID is stored as a flow fingerprint, typically using 32 bits. For $F_{cur}$, $F_{\max}$, and $F_{\min}$, 12 bits are adequate to record the frequency of a flow in a time window with 100K packets. $C_{st}$ can be configured with 8 bits to record the number of additional stable windows accumulated in the recording part. In summary, $S_1$ is 8 bits and $S_2$ is 76 bits. According to our experimental settings, $w$, $M$, and $n$ are typically set to 4096, 1024, and 4, respectively. Consequently, the memory size of SF-Detector is approximately 51KB. In conclusion, SF-Detector is a lightweight method with low space complexity.

\textbf{Time complexity:} For an arrived packet within a flow $f$, we first look up the flow $f$ in the recording part by hashing. This lookup process involves $n$ cells, where $n$ is the number of cells in each bucket of the recording part. If the flow $f$ has been recorded in the recording part, we only update its current-window frequency, which requires $O(n)$ time. Otherwise, we map the flow into the filtering part using its $k$ hash functions and update the corresponding counters, which requires $O(n + k)$ time in the worst case.

At the end of each time window, SF-Detector performs two maintenance procedures. First, it traverses all cells in the recording part to update $F_{\max}$, $F_{\min}$, and $C_{st}$, which requires $O(M \cdot n)$ time. Second, it checks the candidate flows in the filtering part and inserts the verified ones into the recording part. Suppose the number of candidate flows examined at the end of a time window is $U$; this step requires $O(U \cdot (k + n))$ time. Therefore, the online packet-processing cost of SF-Detector is $O(n + k)$ in the worst case, while the end-of-window maintenance cost is $O(M \cdot n + U \cdot (k + n))$. Since $n$ and $k$ are generally configured as very small values (typically $n = 4$ and $k = 3$), SF-Detector remains highly efficient in practice.

\section{Experiments}
This section presents the experimental results of SF-Detector. We first introduce the experimental setup and parameter settings, and then evaluate the performance of SF-Detector by comparing it with prevalent steady-flow detection methods.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\textbf{Datasets:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{CAIDA Dataset:} The CAIDA dataset \cite{b30} was collected by the University of California, San Diego (UCSD). We select 10M packets from the CAIDA2020 and CAIDA2022 traces, which contain 1,245 steady flows when the time window size is set to 1,500 packets.
    \item \textbf{MAWI Dataset:} The MAWI dataset \cite{b31} was collected from daily traces at the transit link of WIDE to the upstream ISP. We select 10M packets, which contain 1,102 steady flows when the time window size is set to 1,500 packets.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Implementation:}
We implement the comparison methods, including SF-Detector, SteadySketch \cite{b23}, CMSketch+, HashTable, and BloomFilter+, in a C++ program. For SteadySketch, we set the ratio of the memory usage of its RBF (Rolling Bloom Filter) to its total memory size to 0.5, while the number of slots in its counter array is determined by the given memory size. For CMSketch+, we implement a Count-Min-Sketch-based method whose number of counters is determined by the given memory size. For HashTable, we implement a basic hash-table-based method without filtering. For BloomFilter+, we implement a Bloom-filter-based method whose number of counters is determined by the given memory size. For SF-Detector, we set the number of counters in its filtering part to 4096 and the number of cells in each bucket of its recording part to 4, while the number of buckets is determined by the given memory size. The filtering part uses three consecutive time windows for pre-verification ($L_f = 3$). We set the fluctuation tolerance threshold $\beta$ to 0.2, the additional consecutive stable-window threshold in the recording part $\Gamma$ to 3, and the time window size to 1,500 packets. Subsequently, we run the program on a server configured with an Intel Core i7 processor and 16GB of memory.

\textbf{Metrics:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Precision Rate (PR):} The ratio of the number of correctly reported steady flows to the total number of reported steady flows.
    \item \textbf{Recall Rate (RR):} The ratio of the number of correctly reported steady flows to the true number of steady flows.
    \item \textbf{F1 Score:} $\frac{2 \cdot PR \cdot RR}{PR + RR}$. The F1 score is the harmonic mean of precision and recall, used to measure the overall accuracy of a method in detecting steady flows.
    \item \textbf{Throughput:} $\frac{N}{T}$, where $N$ is the total number of packets, and $T$ is the total measurement time. Throughput represents million insertions per second (MIPS).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Parameter Settings}
We conducted a comprehensive evaluation of SF-Detector's key parameters, including the number of hash functions $k$, the fluctuation tolerance threshold for steady flow detection $\beta$, and the additional consecutive stable-window threshold $\Gamma$ in the recording part. In our experiments, the filtering part uses three consecutive time windows for pre-verification ($L_f = 3$), with a time window size of 1,500 packets. Experiments were conducted on the CAIDA2022 dataset, and the impact of parameters was evaluated using F1 scores.

\textbf{Effects of $k$:} Experimental results demonstrate the optimal value of $k$ is 3. As allocated memory increases, the F1 score, recall, and precision for all $k$ values exhibit an upward trend, reaching saturation once a certain memory threshold is attained. When $k=3$, the algorithm demonstrates optimal overall performance, with the F1 score exceeding 0.97 at 45KB of allocated memory and stabilising around 0.98 at higher memory allocations.

\section{Conclusion}
Real-time detection of steady flows plays a crucial role in network measurement. To address the issues with existing methods, we propose SF-Detector to detect steady flows with high precision and low memory overhead. We design a hierarchical filtering mechanism that separates potentially steady flow screening via three rotating counting bloom filters from steady-flow verification using a hash-table-based structure. Additionally, we design a stability determination method based on extreme values that avoids complex variance calculations, and a replacement mechanism based on the consecutive-stability counter to address memory overflow.

The experimental results show that SF-Detector achieves up to 97\% precision and 99\% recall rate for detecting steady flows on the CAIDA2020 dataset. Even when the memory size is set to 20KB, the precision and recall rate can achieve up to 86\% and 89\%, respectively. The F1 score of SF-Detector reaches 98\% at 80KB memory, outperforming SteadySketch (95\%), CMSketch+ (82\%), BloomFilter+ (77\%), and HashTable (70\%). These results demonstrate that even under low memory constraints, SF-Detector can ensure high precision, high recall rates, and balanced F1 scores, showing significant advantages over state-of-the-art methods in detecting steady flows. Moreover, SF-Detector achieves higher throughput of approximately 40 MIPS compared to SteadySketch (22 MIPS). This indicates that SF-Detector is capable of real-time detection of steady flows in high-speed network environments.

In our future work, we aim to further optimize SF-Detector to enhance its robustness for detecting steady flows in larger-scale and more complex network environments. Additionally, we plan to extend SF-Detector to support adaptive threshold adjustment for different network scenarios. Simultaneously, we intend to apply SF-Detector to the fields of network capacity planning, traffic engineering, and cache optimization.
